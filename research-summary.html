Native American Food Access and Sovereignty Research Project

Research Summary

Positionality Statement

As a Berkeley student conducting research on Ohlone land, I acknowledge my position as an outsider to Indigenous food sovereignty movements. I have not experienced the intergenerational trauma of land dispossession, forced assimilation, or the disruption of food pathways that continue to impact many Indigenous communities. My research methodology prioritizes transparency and Indigenous knowledge systems, while aiming to (1) uncover inequities, (2) analyze systemic factors, and (3) highlight Indigenous-led solutions. I would be grateful for the opportunity to receive feedback and suggestions for future work from Native American leaders.

Key Findings

My analysis revealed significant disparities in California's food landscape. Most notably:

  • 41.5% of tribal census tracts qualify as food deserts compared to 26.5% of non-tribal tracts
  • The First Nations Food Price Index demonstrates consistently higher food costs on reservations (compared to national averages)

This creates a dual burden of limited physical access and financial barriers to culturally appropriate food.

Root Causes

These disparities stem directly from colonial policies including:

  • Forced relocation and land loss
  • Prohibition of traditional land management practices
  • Disruption of intergenerational knowledge transfer through assimilation policies
  • Problematic federal food distribution programs

Case Studies in Food Sovereignty

These three case studies demonstrate effective pathways to reclaim food sovereignty:

Oneida Nation

Utilizing self-determination contracts to redirect resources toward tribal food systems.

Little Traverse Bay Bands of Odawa

Revitalizing traditional food knowledge alongside improved access.

Karuk Tribe

Reconnecting younger generations to food heritage through educational initiatives.
