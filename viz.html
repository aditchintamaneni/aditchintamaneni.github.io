USDA Food Access Research Atlas Analysis

I used the USDA Food Access Research Atlas (FARA) Dataset to conduct a geospatial analysis of food access disparities across Native American communities and surrounding areas in California. The USDA gives four definitions of "Low-Access" Census Tracts:

  1. Low-income census tracts where a significant number (at least 500 people) or share (at least 33 percent) of the population is greater than one-half mile from the nearest supermarket, supercenter, or large grocery store for an urban area or greater than 10 miles for a rural area. Using this measure, an estimated 53.6 million people, or 17.4 percent of the U.S. population, live in tracts that are low-income and low access and are more than one-half mile or 10 miles from the nearest supermarket.
  2. Low-income census tracts where a significant number (at least 500 people) or share (at least 33 percent) of the population is greater than 1 mile from the nearest supermarket, supercenter, or large grocery store for an urban area or greater than 10 miles for a rural area. This measure shows that an estimated 18.8 million people, or 6.1 percent of the U.S. population, live in low-income and low access tracts and are more than 1 mile or 10 miles from a supermarket.
  3. Low-income census tracts where a significant number (at least 500 people) or share (at least 33 percent) of the population is greater than 1 mile from the nearest supermarket, supercenter, or large grocery store for an urban area or greater than 20 miles for a rural area. Under this measure, an estimated 17.1 million people, or 5.6 percent of the U.S. population, live in low-income and low access tracts and are more than 1 mile or 20 miles from a supermarket.
  4. A fourth and slightly more complex measure incorporates vehicle access directly into the measure, delineating low-income tracts where a significant number of households are located far from a supermarket and do not have access to a vehicle. This measure also includes census tracts with populations that are so remote, that even with a vehicle, driving to a supermarket may be considered a burden because of the great distance.
Source: USDA Economic Research Service

The FARA data is based on a 2019 list of supermarkets, the 2010 Census, and the 2014-2018 American Community Survey. To conduct my analysis, I joined in 2010 California and American Indian/Alaska Native/Native Hawaiian Area (AIANNHA) boundary files. The AIANNH boundary files include boundaries for federal and state-recognized American Indian reservations/off-reservation trust land areas, federal and state tribal designated statistical areas, and joint use areas. For the purposes of my analysis, I have classified them all as "Tribal Areas".

Food Deserts and Tribal Areas in California

This map visualizes food deserts across California census tracts, highlighting tribal area boundaries in blue. I classified a census tract as a food desert if it fits any of the USDA's "Low Access" definitions.

Vehicle Access Issues and Tribal Areas in California

This map visualizes the percentage of households without vehicle access across California census tracts. I calculated this by simply dividing the # of tract housing units without a vehicle by the total # of tract housing units. Unfortunately the FARA data derives the value in the numerator from the 2014-2018 ACS and the value in the denominator from 2010 US Census. This resulted in some percentages being over 100%! I hope to resolve this by replacing the total # of tract housing units with the ACS estimate. I capped the colorbar at 50% for ease of visualization.
